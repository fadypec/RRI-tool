Captured April 2026 for reference during tool development.

## Figure 3-1: Three-Step Assessment Tool

**Step 1:** Does the research create, recreate, or modify a microorganism or toxin?

**Step 2a (if yes to Step 1):** Does the resulting organism exhibit concerning qualities?
- Increased virulence
- Enhanced environmental stability or decontamination resistance
- Novel toxin production capacity
- Improved transmissibility
- Altered host range capability
- Ability to evade natural immunity
- Capacity to interfere with diagnostics, vaccines, or treatments
- Increased horizontal gene transfer potential
- Enhanced dispersal or release capacity
- Suitability as biological weapon

**Step 2b (if no to Step 1):** Can research information, technologies, or products be misused?

**Step 3:** Do released materials pose threats to public health/safety/security, terrestrial or aquatic organisms, environment, economy, or national security?

**Outcome:** High dual-use potential if Step 3 threat exists; otherwise, low potential.

## Hazard Categories

### Tangible Assets
- Biological materials (pathogens, toxins)
- Equipment and tools (agricultural dispersal devices)
- AI-enabled technologies (robotic synthesis, high-throughput screening)
- Research animals (potentially infected specimens)

### Intangible Assets
- Genomic sequences (novel/emerging/high-risk pathogens)
- Virulence gene information
- Pathogen delivery technology details
- Laboratory techniques and procedures
- Organisational biosafety documentation (facility access, engineering specs)

### Activities with Heightened Dual-Use Potential
- Pathogen creation or recreation
- In vivo passage studies (evolution/transmissibility selection)
- Molecular synthesis (de novo creation from sequences)
- Serial passage and gene editing
- Epigenetic manipulation
- Gain-of-function modifications (virulence, transmissibility, host range enhancement)

## Risk Assessment Matrix (Likelihood x Severity)

### Likelihood Factors
- Laboratory condition requirements and feasibility
- Technical skills necessary (including AI reduction potential)
- Resource acquisition (cost, specialisation, regulation, 3D printing potential)
- Cross-application to higher-risk organisms
- Production timeframe acceleration possibilities
- Historical precedent of misuse
- Adversary motivation and evidence of interest

### Severity Factors
- Infectious dose requirements
- Transmissibility between/within species
- Disease severity (serious illness vs. death outcomes)
- Impact on infection control, treatment, surveillance, containment
- Vaccine/treatment availability
- Environmental persistence and ecosystem effects
- Public comprehension and misinformation risk
- Critical infrastructure/economic disruption potential

## Mitigation Strategies

### Experimental Design Modifications
- Pathogen selection alternatives (attenuated strains, auxotrophic variants, inactivated samples, protein components instead of whole agents)
- Surrogate pathogen use (lower pathogenicity alternatives)
- In silico analysis instead of in vivo/in vitro work
- Alternative laboratory techniques (antibody neutralisation vs. live exposure)

### Intangible Asset Protection
- Personnel security screening
- Physical security measures
- SOPs for asset protection
- IT/cybersecurity measures
- Pre-publication review processes

## Key Definition

"Dual-use potential in life science research refers to the qualities of a pathogen or toxin, scientific method, intellectual property, or other related asset that allow it to be either used for legitimate scientific applications or intentionally misused to cause harm or disease."
